It's always a good time to start investing for the simple reason of if not now then when? Of course, it's a good idea to build up an emergency fund first as you don't want a crisis to force you out of your investment positions.

As to ethical funds, you're deferring the judgement of ethics to someone else just as you are deferring financial judgement to someone else in any fund. Unless you can research the fund manager, his philosophies and management strategy thoroughly then all you're going on is the marketing material hence there's no guarantee that the money is truly being invested ethically or responsibly by your standards. I see no point in funds other than indexed funds with varying degrees of beta as most funds are very opaque as to management strategy and some do not specify who their fund manager is other than stating that they have a competent management team. Stay with index funds or better yet learn how to invest yourself. If you want to be socially conscious, budget some philanthropy with the money that you make.

A strategy to deal with unpredictable relative price movements like the exchange rate or even stock market movements in general would be portfolio rebalancing. To illustrate, imagine a $1,000 portfolio where you target 50% to be in cash and the other 50% in a stock, you would start with $500 cash, $500 stock and the stock price drops in half leaving you with $500 cash, $250 stock so you buy $125 stock to rebalance to 50/50, $375 cash, $375 stock. The stock price returns to it's starting price and you have $375 cash, $750 stock for a portfolio value of $1,125 all while the stock price has net zero change in price. This is called volatility pumping and works very well in foreign currencies as currencies allow for fewer transactions to balance a portfolio. That is a portfolio of three currencies with a third of the portfolio value in each currency requires only two or less transactions to rebalance. A portfolio of two stocks and cash with a third in each also requires two trades or less o rebalance but since all trades must to and from cash, it requires on average more trades and hence trading costs then a portfolio of three currencies. With a sufficiently large portfolio, currency variations are just another opportunity to make money through portfolio management.

Agree with John W. The key is to start investing. If you wait for the "right" time, you well never get started. In fact, what feels like the right time is probably when the market has gone up a lot and everyone is positive....which is the wrong time to invest! Use index funds to keep costs to a minimum. That means you keep more of the return and less goes to fund managers. Drip feeding money on a monthly basis gives you "pound cost averaging". In other words, if the market goes down, whilst the fund holding that you currently have will go down, you'll be able to buy more units with your next monthly investment. This is safer than putting big chunks of money into the market in one go.
