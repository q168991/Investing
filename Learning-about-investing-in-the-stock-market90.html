I was in the same position as you: 20 years old and knowing nothing about the stock market. The best way I learned about the stock market and related topics in personal finance are reading really good financial books and blogs. I find if you keep reading (even if they seem repetitive), the things start to stick in your mind. Now, 3 years later, I'm halfway through completing my Certificate for Personal Financial Planning.

A few of my favorites blogs are (you can search for the actual sites with Google) Get Rich Slowly, the Simple Dollar, Generation X Finance, and Five Cent Nickel. Also, look up the Carnival of Investing which is a weekly roundup of interesting investing blog posts and you can find a lot of great blogs from there.

A lot of these blogs also have articles reviewing good (or bad) investing books so that should give you a great head start!

Playing the stock market is like playing poker...you not only play your cards, you play the other players at the table. Many lousy company's stock skyrocket because of investor/analyst hype. Many great compay's stock go nowhere because the company/industry is "boring" to the hyping market makets. To make $$$ in the market, get in on the leading edge of the hype & sell when all newswires and analysts love the stock because soon this stock will crash when the anaysts and marketmakers start to court the next stock. In other words, the stock market is not a numbers game, it's game of reading human nature.

What I did, was read a bit about investing...starting with one of those "dummies" books and moving up to a bit more advanced.

I then set up a notebook for research on various stocks identified in stock screener programs, upgrades, and strong buy ratings, etc.

Then I set up a phony portfolio of my own (you can use MSN stock tracker or do up your own spreadsheet) and made fake trades using my research. I kept track of how often I scored and so on, compared what I expected a stock to do and what it did, etc.

In the end I did this for about 2 years before I was comfortable enough to start with real money.

Whether you are just beginning to save or already have a nest egg for retirement, your money should be working as efficiently and diligently for you as you did to earn it. To succeed in this, however, it is important to start with a solid of understanding of how stock-market investment works.
