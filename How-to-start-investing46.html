You are doing good to start thinking about this at a young age. Here are some basics to consider.

Start with a savings account and put your $300 per month in there until you have 3-6 months of living expenses saved up.

If you have jobs and your employer has a 401-k with matching funds, put in enough to at least get the match.

Start with index mutual funds. Trying to pick individual stocks or bonds without a lot of time spent researching them can lead to bad decisions and you could lose money easily. Don't pay a lot of attention to anyone who claims to have a "hot stock pick".

Keep in mind that the market will have it's ups and downs but over nearly all 10 year periods, it has returned a positive gain. You have 40 years to recover from any short-term down markets so don't worry about them too much.

To be a successful investor, you will need to learn the following. Look for the the following topics in whatever you read:

- The difference between a lending investment (like bonds) and an ownership investment (like stock), and the advantages/disadvantages to each.

- The importance of asset allocation in determining your overall risk and return. In particular, the stock to bond ratio.

- Why do we rebalance our portfolios?

- The impact of costs over long periods of time.

- How past performance is about as useful as reading tea leaves for predicting future performance. Why past performance is not a good reason to pick a mutual fund.

- What is an "Efficient Market", and why is it rare for a mutual fund manager to beat the market average over long periods of time?

- What are Index Funds and why do they prevail most of the time?

- The concept of Reversion-to-the-Mean and its implication to investors.

It is good that you are starting to think about investing at an early age. Many people delay and start to panic as retirement approaches.

The first thing to do is sign up for your company's 401(k), if it offers one. This allows you to reduce your current tax bill by putting pre-tax money into a retirement account. Whatever money you put into this account will be deducted from your taxable income and can grow until you retire, shielded from taxes. Many employers match your contribution up to a certain percentage of income. This money is tax-deferred too. Try to put aside the percentage that your employer will match, as it is like giving yourself an automatic raise.

Next, you should put money into an HSBC Direct or ING Direct account. You get around a 5% return and you do not need to worry about your investment going down in value. This money you can set aside for major purchases, like buying a house, or for rainy days. You can access your money easily and you earn much more money in those accounts than you would in a bank's saving account.

Once you have a cushion, you should look at index funds. Vanguard has low fees, but the initial investment is often high. You should probably get a fund that tries to match the performance of the S&P500 index, which is a good measure of the US stock market performance. You should also invest about 15% of your money in an index that tracks international markets. By investing in both markets, you reduce your risk of losing money and your risk-adjusted return is higher than investing in the US market alone.

Kiplinger magazine often lists "Best Mutual Funds." You can begin picking some of those as you have more money set aside. You should probably buy some mutual funds in an IRA account, which is another way to reduce your taxes and let your money grow for your retirement.

You should begin buying individual stocks only after you have a good deal of money set aside for present-day expenditures and only after you feel comfortable investing.

Good luck and I hope you have fun.
