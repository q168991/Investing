Everyone has a different version of what a "good amount" is. Make sure you have enough in savings in case things go south for at least 6 months. As to minimum to invest, it depends on where you go. Schwab allows you to invest with as little as $100 if you contribute in every month. For others like Vanguard for many funds they need at least $3000.
Some of the best are Vanguard, T. Rowe Price, Fidelity, and Schwab. Avoid the big banks like the plague. Don't let them rip you off with loads (sales charges) and fees. Check how much the company charges you as an expense ratio. A good one might charge you 0.2-0.8 %. If they charge more than 1% than go somewhere else. And if they charge any kind of 12b-1 fee, hold on to your wallet and RUN.

But some basic generic advice is:


Have a goal - know what you are investing for, and choose the right investment to achieve it.

Choose the right amount of risk - neither too cautious nor too reckless.

Do your research - choose what's right for you - not someone else, especially a sales rep.

Be diversified and allocate your investments properly.

Thank long term and avoid the Big Money Fast hype.

Don't invest according to emotion - especially fear/panic.

Don't just look at the investment - consider fees/taxes/commissions around it.

For stocks typically you are talking about at least a 5 year investment period. If less, consider getting into bonds or a bond fund instead.
If you want to get into the market but don't know what stock to pick, I would choose an index fund or ETFs. Instead of throwing all your eggs into one basket (one company), index funds can invest you in dozens, hundreds, or thousands of companies all at once and so there is less risk. This protects you if any one company or industry runs into trouble. For bonds, the returns are less, but more solid.

If you are thinking of retirement, consider a Roth IRA. Your money grows tax free, and when you retire you can withdraw it tax free as well.

Getting individual stocks make more sense if you really want to buy stock at a place you work at, or want to really get involved and are the hands-on investor type. If you want to be more passive and have things grow over time, index funds make more sense. If you want to see some of the variety out there,
