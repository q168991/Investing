An index fund already diversifies your risk, the more wide ranging an index fund, the more diversified the investment. For example, an S&P 500 index fund (say SPY etf) invests in a weighted portfolio made up of the 500 companies that make up the S&P index.

There is no advantage to looking for a fund with a $10,000 minimum. An index fund invests its money based on weights and specific stocks and your $10,000 has no more influence than my investment of $100or any less influence than my investment of $1,000,000. The $1,000,000 fund invests in exactly the same stocks and proportionally the same percent of shares as the $100,000,000 fund, so they each go up and down the same and your $10,000 goes up and down the same in either fund.

In any case, your money is as safe as the particular index you are investing in. If you invest in the S&P 500 and the S&P prices go down, your investment loses money. However, it is less likely that with a 500 stock fund, you will see drops or increases that would be as varied as say a Dow Transportation index fund such as the iShare Transportation etf - these sector specific funds invest only in stocks in a particular sector and their fortunes rise and fall with the sector itself, not the overall market, so if transportations are down, maybe due to gas prices, while the S&P 500 is up, maybe due to the profits from gas prices, you would lose if you are in the transportation fund while you would make money in the S&P 500.

Index funds generally perform at the same level as the stock index they track. So, you are doing no better or no worse than that market. It's another way of saying mediocrity. The goal of investing is usually to beat the market, not do exactly what the market is doing.

They perform exactly like the index they are intended to duplicate. Whether it is safe or not depends on the index - I would stick with the S&P 500 or Russell 2000 indexes. Could you still lose money? Of course, if the market goes down.
