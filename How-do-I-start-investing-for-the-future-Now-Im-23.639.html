You have plenty of answers suggesting a 401k or a Roth IRA. They are good for retirement savings, especially the Roth; but if you should need access to the funds before then you are better off keeping some of your investments outside of those vehicles. When you talk about the Google gravy train, you are talking a lot of specific risk. There certainly are and will be more gravy trains coming down the main line. The trouble is telling them from the locals and those engineered by the Casey Jones of the world. It is best to avoid a train wreck if you can. Not always possible. I have been in quite a few over the years, but survived all of them with only minor injuries though a few certainly put me out of commission for a while.

Quite frankly a $1000 return in two years on a $2000 investment is pretty darn good. The trick is holding on to it. How much of that did the government latch on to.

$3000 is not a lot of money when it comes to buying stocks. It does not allow for much diversity of investments.

The future Googles of the world are most likely found among the small cap stocks. That is where you are most likely to find the choicest of express gray trains. The only problem is that there are over 5000 small cap stocks to choose from. What happens to many is they will have a good run for one or two years and then derail. Other places you might look are Chinese and Indian stocks. Both countries are growing about 3x faster than the U S and both have a lot of catching up to do infastructure wise. They both rely heavily on rail transportation so the gravy train should keep on track. Should be a lot of opportunies there.

Some investors suggest also Russia, but I don't like their train schedules one bit. Too likely to be switched to a siding for a long period of time or wind up on a dead end siding.

The more you do trading, the clearer it will become that you cannot reliably make money unless you are diversified. But the dillema becomes how can I make a lot of money if I am diversified? Right?

Choose an index fund or even a mutual fund that is leveraged. And choose a fund that is a diversified emerging markets fund. Historically, these are the highest returning funds in existience. Over the past 12 months, UUPIX has returned about 120%. But over the long run average it averages 30% per year. This is triple the general stock market. The volatility is high, but nothing comparable to individual stocks. This is a long term investment. The longer your horizon, the higher your expected return should be.

Don't go pretending you can beat the market by outsmarting it buying and shorting individual stocks, unless you have a large diversified portfolio. This is because otherwise your trading costs will be too high to make any money. The sad truth is those whom are not diversified cannot withstand the losses that the diversified can. So as a consequence, the undiversified investor will usually end up essentially folding his position at a loss because he cannot wait years for that single company to make a comeback.

You should really look into a Roth IRA account. You can put however much a month you want into it, and by the time you're retirement comes around, will you have all this money waiting for you. Also, for each dollar, you get $.50 back when it's tax season. So that makes for a nice tax return also. My husband and I are 21 and 25 and just opened one a few months ago. Also when you've had it for I believe 6 months, they send you some information on investing money into other areas. Hope this helped!

Mutual Fund: You should know the meaning of mutual funds, before you choose to invest in mutual funds. These funds are a type of security that can be traded on the stock market, allowing shareholders to buy and sell shares in the funds. The revenue generated by purchase of shares is used by mutual fund manager to buy more shares of specific stocks, bonds, and other market securities and money market instruments.

Since the prices of the stocks, bonds, and other securities held by the mutual fund vary, the value of the fund changes. The average value of every share of the mutual fund is fixed daily based on the total value of the underlying securities held by the fund.

This involves the shareholders of a mutual fund directly with their investment as against those who just buy individual securities and observe as the prices fluctuate.
