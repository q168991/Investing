When you start, keep it simple.

You keep it simple and effective when you:
1) Invest in index funds (like the SPY for the S&P 500)
2) Buy the funds when the long-term trend in the market starts to go up and sell when the long-term trend in the market starts to go down.

Once you get comfortable with putting your real money to work in this way, you could start thinking of doing something else.

If you have sufficient time available and the skills and passion to analyze the financial data of individual companies, you could start allocating a part of your investments to individual stocks. But do your own analysis and do not follow the penny stock picks of someone else. And always keep an eye on the long-term market trend. Because when the overall market is going down like in 2008 or 2001-2003, most stocks go down, even those of the great companies.

Note that I do not bother with trying to pick individual stocks since I am happy with the returns I can get with the combination of Trend Investing and Index Funds.

I would strongly suggest you incorporate some kind of technicals into your trading. I'm a trend trader and use an ADX (average directional index) strategy that works quite well. So it could be ADX or moving averages or whatever, but applying some kind of technical indicator to your stock picks can be extremely successful.

The attributes of stocks aren't always what propels them upward, but technicals will tell you what the stock price is doing right now and the strength of any movement. Additionally, you can also use ADX to determine the overall market trend which will really give your positions power.

My trades are based on some basic stock characteristics like average volume and price and then candidates are picked solely on how the price is moving. The idea is to buy into a trending stock and sell when the price breaks down. If the stock has some redeeming attributes - great - the more probabilities in your favor, the better.

You'll need to understand risk and it's not the simple risk reward ratio that Markowitz popularized in the efficient frontier aka the Markowitz bullet and the standard VaR (Value at Risk) reports. Such a straight ratio would have you mortgaging your house to buy LottoMax tickets every time it hit $50 million plus a few Maxmillions (I'm guessing you're in Canada since you referred to RRSP's). The problem with the high reward low probability outcome is called the St. Petersburg Paradox and the solution proposed in 1738 by Daniel Bernoulli in his paper "Exposition of a New Theory on the Measurement of Risk" which was translated into English from Latin and published in a 1954 issue of Econometrica, was to assume a suitable utility of wealth, the one he used is the log utility of wealth. An example of this would be if you had the opportunity to invest as many times as you wanted in a favourable fair coin toss where if you won the 50/50 coin toss you would win back twice your wager plus receive your wager back but it you lost you would lose your wager, such an investment opportunity would have an expected return of $1.50 on the $1 so how could you screw up such an opportunity. You can screw it up by over betting, if you wagered all that you had with each coin toss, you stand to gain the most but you're guaranteeing that you'll lose everything with the first loss. So how much do you wager with each coin toss? You work this out by maximizing the geometric mean of outcomes, basically if W is your net worth and X is your wager, then you want to size your bet to maximize the geometric mean of outcomes given by:

e^( 0.5 * ln( W + 2 * X ) + 0.5 * ln( W - X ) )

and you should only invest if the resulting value is greater then e^( ln( W ) ) or just W which presumably is the guaranteed risk free alternative of not investing (it may be a good idea to replace that with a risk free investment alternative such as the US treasury bond or to use a required rate of return such as the rate of inflation).

This is basically a two outcome scenario and amounts to:

e^( (probability of first outcome) * ln( your wealth if first outcome occurs) + (probability of second outcome ) * ln( your wealth if second outcome occurs) )

Hence it can be generalized to any number of possible outcomes hence concurrent opportunities and to opportunities with multiple outcomes. In the binary case of a win lose coin toss, it simplifies to the Kelly Criterion which quickly tells you that the wager X should be 25% of your wealth W in this case for optimal growth of wealth. The Kelly criterion is named for John L Kelly a physicist at Bell Labs who in a 1956 paper, described the application of Claude Shannon's Information Theory which is the basis of digital telecommunication to a fictitious horse race.

The interesting thing about the geometric approach is that it avoids over betting and it avoids total loss.
