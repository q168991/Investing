To begin investing, I often recommend avoiding a broker, unless you are comfortable paying broker fees. If you are investing anything under 10,000 dollars, I would suggest broad-indexed mutual funds, like the S&P 500 or similar. If you are looking to start with small amounts, maybe consider broad ETFs. You just want to make sure to diversify and not put it all in one place. US Large Caps, maybe some small caps, Europe and a hint of something else. I agree with the above responder (even though his name is pathetic).

Definitely put some time and research into it before you get started. I would tune into CNBC, follow financial news such as the Wall Street Journal or Morningstar, read some books on simple stock investing, margin, options, ETFs, bonds, and so on. If you put a few days into it you'll get the general idea in no time.

Then research for a brokerage firm. I do it online (online is better than in person... but many brokerage firms allow clients to come into the office and discuss trades for free... it all depends on the broker you use) through Schwab and TradeKing. I find Schwab offers very good research material (comparable to Fidelity I've heard), but their commissions are high compared to TradeKing. Therefore, I research the material on Schwab and trade on TradeKing :D.

Good luck, don't get too risky... I've definitely had my fair share of burns.
