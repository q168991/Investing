The guidelines are quite vague and unsubstantiated, the usual advice that you would get when you're young is to be aggressive and often the young will invest in a 100% growth or a 95% growth portfolio because of this. However you will have your investments for a long time and hence it's almost assured that your portfolio will have to weather a downturn or two which typically happens at least once every decade. When you have a 100% portfolio, there's nothing to be done during a downturn except hold on for the ride so there is no way for you to mitigate the downturn and benefit from the bargain prices that it presents. For this reason, the likes of Ben Graham and Claude Shannon advocate a 50/50 portfolio.

The key behind a 50/50 portfolio is that 50% is kept relatively risk free in bonds and cash equivalents which means that when the market goes down, you can take advantage of the downturns by simply rebalancing back to the 50/50 position. It acts like a one way valve so that your fortunes gain with the market when it's going up and doesn't lose too much when the market is going down. Shannon's infamous lecture on the 50/50 portfolio has been called "Shannon's Demon" in reference to the concept of "Maxwell's Demon" in thermodynamics because of the one way effect on entropy. The 50/50 portfolio and rebalancing harvests volatility and the one thing you know when you're young is that you'll see both ups and downs in the market.

If you go with the usual advice of aggressive, the only thing you are assuring is that you will not be able to take advantage of the few spectacular sales that will come your way during your accumulation phase. The gains from scooping up bargains during market downturns are literally once in a decade opportunities and you'd be giving up on these extraordinary opportunities.

Another thing people will talk about is risk tolerance, as if the increase in reward for increased risk always justifies the risk so long as you can tolerate it, this is not the case. There is a limit beyond which the additional risk is not worth the additional reward. Imagine if you played Russian roulette with a thousand chamber gun, one bullet and spinning the chambers after each pull of the trigger, you'll still be dead after the first loss, the game may be worth pointing the gun at your leg but pointing it at your temple is assuming too much risk.

If you define your objective as comfort, then risk tolerance should be considered, if you define your objective as optimal growth of your portfolio then if you know the probabilities of the outcomes, you can actually calculate the optimal risk to assume. The method was described by Daniel Bernoulli in his 1738 paper "Exposition of a New Theory on The Measurement of Risk" and was rediscovered by John L Kelly (Kelly Criterion) in 1956 when applying Claude Shannon's information theory to gambling and investing. Kelly used gambling examples which simplified the derivation of a simple equation due to the binary nature of gambling wagers, the well defined probabilities and the well defined outcomes so at first glance the equation seems to only apply to sizing a bet for a binary wager but if you step back to the concept of optimizing the geometric mean outcome, you'll see that it applies to portfolio construction (concurrent Kelly) as well as multiple outcomes. The interesting thing is that is you take 12% per year as a proxy for the usual expected return from investing in a company (economists use 12% as a proxy for stock market returns), 2% per year as the probability of total loss (based on the average Fortune 500 company having a life expectancy of 40 years) and 8% per year as the probability of a market downturn of about 60% loss then you would want to invest X percentage of your portfolio in stocks while keeping the rest risk free (cash, bonds or cash equivalents) such that you maximize the following geometric mean of outcomes:

e^( 0.9 * ln( 1 - X + X * 1.12 ) + 0.08 * ln( 1 - X + X * 0.4 ) + 0.02 * ln( 1 - X ) )

which would be at a maximum of 1.01025 when X is 0.45 so given those assumptions about stock market returns which are reasonable assumptions, the optimal portfolio growth would be when 45% is invested in the stock and 55% kept as cash.

Obviously, better returns are possible, a probability distribution function should be used, the probability of a total loss can be addressed with diversification etc. so it's quite possible for the optimal percentage to be higher. The famous investor Ben Graham advises no more than 80% and no less than 25%, suggesting just shy of 50% to be the most prudent choice, you'll notice that as aggressive as Warren Buffett is, he's never fully invested.

You'll note that this mathematically and experience based estimates for optimal portfolio construction tends to correspond with the moderate choice not the aggressive growth choice.
