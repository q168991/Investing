They are average % return over 3, 5, and 10 years and not compounded. Just add the % return for each year and divide by the number of years involved. If the fund lost 50% the first year, then it would take a 100% return the next year to be even. So if the account had 10K had the following returns:
Year 1: +10
Year 2: -20
Year 3: +15
Year 4: +5
Year 5: +20
Total: +30 or 30/5 = 6% average over 5 years and account would grow to $13,382

But in reality based on the actual returns annually here follows how the account did:
Year 1: +10, $11,000
Year 2: -20, $8,800
Year 3: +15, $10,120
Year 4: +5, $10,626
Year 5: +20, $12,751 which is less than the average return calculation. This lower amount is based upon a compounded annual yield of 4.9806% over 5 years. The compounded yield would be the appropriate number to report but is not.

The mutual fund industry uses the average returns to deceive potential shareholders into investing in mutual funds since it gives a false higher return number.
