Bonds are you lending your money to a company or government, which will pay you back dividends on it for using your money. They are more stable, but if a company does very well and is making a lot of profit, you don't share in it - you only get what the bond is pledged to pay you.

Typically people buy bonds when:

1) There is a low tolerance for risk.

2) The period of investment is less than 5 years.

3) The markets are chaotic or going down.

4) Getting dividends is more important than long term growth.

A lot depends on how you want to invest your money. Like stocks, there are more conservative as well as more aggressive investments - but bonds are less volatile than stocks. And contrary to what you might have heard, bonds can carry risk as well, such as risk of default, and inflation. Government bonds are safest, but naturally have the lowest returns. High Yield Corporate bonds are essentially better grade junk-bonds with more risk. If you are interested in the former, try looking at ***savingsbond.gov
But if you want something with better returns with low to moderate risk, look at GNMA, a Total Bond Index Fund, various tax-exempt bond funds (especially if you are in a higher tax bracket), or the High Yield Corporate. Some discount places are Vanguard, T. Rowe Price, Fidelity, and Schwab.
Generally if you are not close to retirement and have more than 10 years to invest, stocks are often a better choice and will outperform bonds over time. You might consider a more conservative stock investment (which also pays dividends, like around 1.75%) such as the Total Stock Market Infex Fund or S&P 500 Fund. Pick a place with a no-load and low fees, like Vanguard, T. Rowe Price, Fidelity, and Schwab. Avoid the big banks like the plague. Don't let them rip you off with loads (sales charges) and fees. Check how much the company charges you as an expense ratio. A good one might charge you 0.2-0.8 %. If they charge more than 1% than go somewhere else.
