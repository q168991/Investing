Real estate is a concentrated risk, it's a high proportion of your net worth that you're investing and if it pans out then great but if it doesn't then tough. One thing about investing is that it is placing your money at risk.

A game theory example would be if you had the opportunity to wager on fair coin tosses with a payout of 2 to 1, that is if you win, you win twice your wager plus your wager is returned to you but if you lose, you lose your wager. Now it's clear that if you wager nothing, you risk nothing and you will gain nothing and it's also clear that if you wager everything that you have, although you stand to gain the most when you win, you will lose everything when you lose. So what is the optimal fraction of your money to place at risk? With the coin toss, that can be calculated and it's 25%. With real life investments, the outcomes and probabilities can only be estimated so it's much harder and requires some judgement to determine how much you should risk but it's definitely not everything.

With real estate, it's far too easy to risk too much. For some the risk pans out so there are plenty of success stories but over betting is a sure way to ruin good investment opportunities and in reality, real estate produces more horror stories that you never hear about then success stories. However there are ways to limit your risk, such as REIT's.

Stocks is the same situation, it's quite easy to over bet but it's also very easy to be conservative with stocks and assure a reasonable outcome.

Starting a business has tremendous return potential, the retail business model is typically a 40% markup so if it takes a year to move stock, and neglecting operating expenses, you have a 40% return. But businesses take effort. Stocks are investing in the efforts of others, you share a small part of another entrepreneurs gains without having to contribute the work. With your own business, you'll find that all the work comes from you and indeed most entrepreneurs work years on end for what amounts to pennies per hour just to keep their businesses alive.

If you've maxed out both the $5,000 per year contribution limit to your IRA and the $16,500 401k contribution limit then you contribute to a brokerage account which is not tax sheltered however you can leverage it to effectively extend the tax shelter by holding municipal bonds and long term growth stocks which do not generate annual taxable income in your brokerage account and keep your portfolio rebalance point in your tax sheltered accounts.

Never consider touching the investment budget for anything other than investment. If there isn't enough in one of the other categories then you can't afford it, plain and simple.
