You're right about investing instead of clothes and electronics but you're wrong about gold and silver being viable investments.

Yes the price of gold has risen to a value of $1,652 today and this rise has been recent but if you look back 30 years, you would've bought it at $850 per ounce in the 80's giving you a gain of 2.24% per year. Even if you had bought it at $300 after the collapse in the 80's, you would've made 5.85% per year all while the stock market was making 18% to 22% and inflation was 4.5%. And of course silver has reportedly been lagging gold but I have no estimates of what it might've been 30 years ago.

Then there's the spread. With Gold it's typically 40%, indeed one dealer is offering 10 Oz of gold for $24,570 which works out to $2,470 an ounce, a 49.5% spread. With Silver, a dealer is offering an ounce for $59.99 when the spot rate is $35.315 which is a 69.9% spread. You pay this spread both ways when you buy and sell from and to a dealer so you're typically losing as much as 75% of your value investing in the above Gold and 91% of your value with the above silver, all without the prices changing at all. It's certainly not a good investment to lose 91% of your value immediately before the rates even changes. Gainsville does seem to have silver at the spot rate or lower but many of their items are not available, how likely is it that you'll get what you want and how verifiable is the bullion? Most dealers charge a spread because they can, if someone is quoting the spot rate or lower on the bullion as Gainsville is, you need to find out why. I don't know enough about Gainsville to tell why they are below spot.

Without the spread, metals a poor investment, with a spread even a small one, it's a money loser. You're better off with a even a secure 25% equity, 75% safer mutual fund which is safer then a 100% bond fund then you are investing in metals. If you're cautious, a 50/50 fund should have the same risk as a 100% bond fund, if you're brave a 80% equity fund should be the limit, a 100% equity fund can not benefit from the bargains of a downturn. You can have a gold or silver ETF as a small portion of your portfolio to diversify against systematic risk but that's like building a shelter against a comet hitting the Earth, it won't do much to help you.

Five easy ways to start collecting physical silver

1. Morgan Silver Dollars

The Morgan Silver Dollars are by far the most widely collected United States Coin. The dollars which are 90% silver and contain nearly an ounce of the precious metal were struck from1878 to 1904 and then in 1921, were produced in the hundreds of millions far more than ever needed for the time period.

2. American Silver Eagle
The American Silver Eagles have been the official Silver coin of the United States since 1986. The demand for these coins has increased significantly in the last few years. These 1 ounce .9993 Silver coins not only hold silver value but a collector value as well.

3. Junk Silver
Junk silver refers to coins with 90% silver and 10% copper content. Because junk silver coins have value as bullion, legal tender, and at times as collectibles, pre-1965 silver coinage really is an excellent value for anybody wanting to keep some silver stocked away.

4. Silver Art Bars

Silver Art Bars are usually .999 pure silver bars sold in 1 thru 100oz increments the minimum value of the silver art bar is its raw silver value (the current melt price). The Silver Art Bars also carry additional value depending on the rarity of the silver art bar and its popularity within the collecting community.

5. Silver Rounds

Silver round are coin-shaped .999 pure silver bullion made by private mints not backed by any government for the purpose of silver collecting and investment. They are basically the same as the silver art bars as far as having both silver and collection value.

If you're convinced that buying silver is the right thing to do, I suggest three things.

First, be very good at doing math. You'll need it to be able to accurately calculate the amount of silver in a purchase.

Second, learn the specifications for US silver coins made before 1965, half dollars made from 1965-1970, and nickels made 1942 - 1945 (with the exception of those with the D mint mark, which are regular composition).

Third, start buying these coins. You will pay much lower premiums for the silver than you will buying Eagles, Maple Leafs and other one ounce bullion.

Once you become more knowledgeable about calculating the silver value using the specifications and the current 'spot' price, you can expand into two other areas. Foreign silver coins, and collectable silver such as Franklin Mint and other private mints. Do your research to know what's in them, and you can buy silver for much closer to spot than bullion coins, sometimes even below spot.

As for where to buy, avoid companies in the business of selling precious metals to avoid the highest premiums. Search eBay, and scour the local estate sales. I've found some of my best deals at estate sales with private mint stuff, when I knew exactly what I was looking at and my competition didn't.

I use APMEX to buy precious metals. They do not require a minimum purchase and they usually have American Eagles and Canadian Maples on hand. Some other sites will have your items on backorder for over 3 months because they do not have the silver on hand.

Gold and silver are good investments to make but don't expect to make much money on them right away. They are a safe investment and will always have the same value in terms of buying power regardless of factors that effect the value of the dollar such as inflation. These metals are used more as a safeguard against economic crisis.
